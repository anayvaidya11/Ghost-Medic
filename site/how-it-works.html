How it works

Four parts, one line of text between them.

Sensing happens on the wrist. Thinking happens in the pack. Speaking happens wherever you can hear it. Between them runs one line of plain text, ten times a second. This page walks the whole path and stops at each point where something is standing in for the real thing.

01 · The wrist unit

Three sensors, and one thing it refuses to report

The wrist unit carries three parts that each answer a different question. It is programmed in C that runs directly on the chip, with no operating system underneath it.

The partWhat it measuresWhat that is good for
BMP280Air pressure and temperatureAir thins predictably as you climb, so pressure gives you altitude. The temperature is the air around the device, not your body.
LIS3DHAcceleration, three directionsMotion, and the particular signature of a fall.
MAX30102Light reflected off skinRaw light readings, and deliberately nothing more. See below.

Height from air pressure

Air gets thinner the higher you go, predictably enough that you can run the relationship backwards: measure the pressure, calculate the height. This is the line of firmware that does it.

float bmp280_pressure_to_altitude(float pressure_pa) {
    return 44330.0f *
        (1.0f - powf(pressure_pa / BMP280_SEA_LEVEL_PA, 0.1903f));
}

Take the pressure you just measured, compare it to standard pressure at sea level, and the standard atmospheric formula turns that ratio into metres.

The catch, which the code and this site both state: "standard pressure at sea level" is a fixed number, and real sea-level pressure moves with the weather. So this figure is trustworthy for change, meaning "you have climbed 300 metres", and not for absolute elevation. It is not GPS and does not pretend to be.

What a fall looks like to a machine

While you are falling, an accelerometer reads close to nothing, because in free fall nothing is pushing against it. Then you land and it reads a sharp spike. Either signal on its own is ordinary: sitting down hard gives you the spike, waving your arm gives you the lull. The firmware only calls it a fall when it sees both, in that order, within 400 milliseconds.

Label

This is a demonstration, not a medical-grade fall detector. The thresholds are textbook values that have never been tuned against real falls, and the source code says so in the same words.

No heart rate

The optical sensor shines light through your skin and measures what comes back. That signal does contain a pulse, but extracting it takes signal processing this project does not implement. So the site shows the raw light readings and calls them exactly that.

This is not a footnote. It is enforced in four places: the firmware only reports counts, the app has no field to put a heart rate in, the text sent to the model explicitly forbids inferring one, and a test fails if a heart rate ever appears. An invented vital sign is worse than no vital sign, because someone might act on it.

02 · The link

A cable doing a radio's job

In the finished idea, the wrist unit talks to the pack over a short-range radio. That radio does not exist yet. What exists instead is a small program that reads lines over a wire and hands them to anything that asks for them. Same job, minus the radio.

It is deliberately stupid. It does not interpret, reshape, average, or fix anything. A line goes in and the identical line comes out. That is what makes the substitution honest: the parts downstream cannot tell whether a line came from a board, the browser simulator, or a recording, because there is genuinely no difference in what arrives.

It drops exactly one thing. When the board powers on it prints a greeting reporting which sensors woke up, and that greeting has no timestamp. Anything without a timestamp is not a reading, so it never reaches the rest of the system.

03 · The pack computer

The model is told what the sensors saw, and what not to conclude

The language model is ordinary and off the shelf: a small open model running locally with no internet connection. The engineering is not the model. It is what the model is told, and what it is forbidden from doing.

Every question sent to it carries a block of sensor readings written out in words, with each qualification attached to the reading it qualifies:

DERIVED READINGS:
- Altitude: 2.1 m (pressure-derived vs a fixed 101325 Pa sea-level
  reference; reliable for RELATIVE change only — NOT GPS or true elevation)
- Temperature: 21.0 °C (AMBIENT air at the device — NOT body temperature)
- Fall detected: YES (accelerometer free-fall→impact heuristic,
  not medical-grade)

RAW SIGNALS (non-diagnostic):
- Optical counts: red 10249, ir 10398. These are raw photodiode counts
  from the optical sensor. No pulse-extraction algorithm runs on this
  device: heart rate and blood-oxygen saturation (SpO2) are NOT
  available and must not be inferred from these numbers.

Each number arrives with its own limits attached, so the model cannot treat the altitude as GPS or the air temperature as a fever. The last paragraph does the most work: it tells the model, inside the request itself, that the light readings are not vital signs and must not be turned into a pulse.

If a sensor has failed, its line reads "unavailable" rather than zero. A zero is a reading. "Unavailable" is the truth.

That is the real output of services/sensorContext.ts, wrapped here to fit the column and shown without its opening and closing markers. The app can show you the exact bytes it sent, on request.

One rule sits above all of it: if the readings disagree with what the person says, the person wins. A sensor on a wrist knows less than someone looking at their own leg.

When the wristband speaks first

If the accelerometer sees the signature of a fall, the assistant does not wait to be asked. It starts the conversation itself, with guidance for someone who may have just hit their head. That behaviour needs one guard.

const risingEdge = fallDetected && !prevFall;
prevFall = fallDetected;

if (!risingEdge) return false;
if (suppressed) return false; // consumed, not queued
if (nowMs - lastFiredMs < opts.cooldownMs) return false;

The wristband reports ten times a second, so one fall arrives as a long run of identical "fall detected" readings. Firing on each one would ask the model forty questions about a single stumble.

So it fires on the moment the answer changes from no to yes, not on every yes, then stays quiet for thirty seconds. One fall, one question. Measured against a live model during a looping replay: two requests thirty seconds apart, where the naive version would have made about sixty.

04 · The interface

Six steps, and one line that matters more than the rest

The answer comes back as at most six steps, one sentence each, because the person reading it may be frightened, cold, and alone. It always ends with a line about evacuation, meaning whether to call for rescue, and the app pulls that line out and displays it separately. It is the most consequential thing on the screen.

The screen keeps two kinds of number apart. Derived values, meaning altitude, temperature, and whether a fall was seen, are conclusions the system drew. Raw values, meaning the light readings and the acceleration, are shown as what they are with no interpretation. A reading that failed shows a dash, never a zero.

Placeholders

You can hold a button and record your voice, and the recording is real, but nothing turns it into text yet. You can photograph a wound, and the photo is real, but nothing looks at it yet. Both are wired up and do nothing, and are marked as such in the code.

The path that works end to end is typed text plus sensor readings, to the local model, to a parsed and spoken answer.

One more thing about the interface

The answer appears on screen word by word, which looks like the model thinking aloud. It is not. The complete answer arrives first and the app reveals it at a readable pace. Real word-by-word generation is separate work that has not been done.
