Hardware

The wrist unit, routed down to the last trace.

A two-layer board carrying an RP2040 and three sensors: 35 components, 477 copper segments, 63 vias, powered and programmed over one USB-C connector. I drew the schematic, placed every part, and routed every trace by hand in KiCad. The files are in the repository.

Two things the render does not show

The optical sensor has no body in the picture. Its package has no 3D shape in the standard library, so the exporter leaves that spot empty. It is in the schematic, in the layout, and routed. The gap is left visible rather than filled with a stand-in part.

The copper is under the surface, as it is on a real board. The routing is the next section.

The routing

Every connection placed by hand, one at a time

477 segments routed by hand between 35 components, plus 63 vias. A via is a plated hole letting a connection cross to the other side of the board.

Why two layers

Copper on both faces, nothing between. More layers make routing easier but cost money, and this design does not need them.

The front carries the signals above. The back is almost entirely one sheet of copper, 5,218 of the board's 5,561 square millimetres, held at zero volts. That gives every part a short, quiet path back to the supply, the single most effective way to stop a mixed-signal board misbehaving.

The decisions

Why each part is on the board

The processor and the memory chip beside it

An RP2040, the same processor as in a Raspberry Pi Pico, because the firmware was already written for it. The bare chip has nowhere to store its program, so a separate memory chip sits beside it. The crystal's frequency matches what the programming toolkit expects by default, so nothing needs reconfiguring.

Three sensors, two wires

All three sensors share one pair of wires. Each has its own address and the processor calls them one at a time, so three need no more wiring than one. Two resistors hold the wires at a known level when nobody is speaking. The pins match the firmware's, so board and software agree without either being adjusted.

One connector for everything

A USB-C socket carries both power and the program, so there is nothing else to plug in. USB delivers 5 volts and the board wants 3.3, so a regulator steps it down. Hold one button, tap the other, and the board appears as a drive you can drop a file onto. A four-pin header is the second way in, for when the first stops working.
