Hardware

The wrist unit, routed down to the last trace.

A two-layer board carrying an RP2040 and three sensors: 35 components, 477 copper segments, 63 vias, powered and programmed over one USB-C connector. The schematic and layout files are in the repository and open in KiCad, which is free.

Two things the render does not show

The optical sensor has no body in the picture. Its package has no 3D shape in the standard component library, so the exporter leaves that spot empty. It is in the schematic, in the layout, and routed. It just has no shape to draw. Rather than substitute a stand-in part, the gap is left visible.

The copper is under the surface. The green coating covers the routing on a board, and it covers it here too. The routing itself is the next section.

The routing

Every connection placed one at a time

This is the front copper: 477 individual segments carrying power and signals between 35 components, plus 63 vias. A via is a small plated hole that lets a connection pass through to the other side of the board when its path is blocked.

Why two layers

The board has copper on both faces and nothing in between. Boards can have four, six, or twelve layers, and more layers make routing easier, but each one costs money and this design does not need them.

The two faces do different jobs. The front carries the signals you just looked at. The back is almost entirely one continuous sheet of copper, 5,218 of the board's 5,561 square millimetres, held at zero volts. That sheet gives every part a short, quiet path back to the power supply, which is the single most effective thing you can do to stop a mixed-signal board from misbehaving.

A criticism of my own layout

At 77.5 × 71.75 mm this board is far too big to wear on a wrist. The components take up a fraction of it and the rest is empty. It was routed for correctness rather than size, and shrinking it to something wearable is real work that has not been done. Calling it a wrist unit describes the intent, not the current dimensions.

The decisions

Why each part is on the board

The processor and the memory chip beside it

The chip in charge is an RP2040, the same processor as in a Raspberry Pi Pico, chosen because the firmware was already written for it. Using the bare chip instead of a ready-made Pico module means one extra part is needed: a separate memory chip to hold the program, because the bare processor has nowhere to store it. A small quartz crystal next to it keeps time, and its frequency matches what the programming toolkit expects by default, so nothing has to be reconfigured.

Three sensors, two wires

All three sensors share the same pair of wires. Each sensor has its own address and the processor calls them by name one at a time, so three sensors need no more wiring than one. Two resistors hold those wires at a known level when nobody is speaking. The two pins used are the same two the firmware was written for, so the software and the board agree without either being adjusted.

One connector for everything

A USB-C socket supplies power and carries the program onto the board, so there is nothing else to plug in. USB delivers 5 volts and everything on the board wants 3.3, so a regulator steps it down. Two buttons handle programming: hold one, tap the other, and the board appears on your computer as a drive you can drop a file onto. A four-pin header offers a second way in, for the case where the first way stops working, which is the situation you cannot fix later.
