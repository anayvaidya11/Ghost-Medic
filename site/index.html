Prototype

A first-aid assistant that works with no signal.

Ghost Medic is a prototype for people who are hours from help and have no connection. A unit on the wrist tracks movement and the air around you. A language model running on hardware in your own pack reads those numbers together with what you tell it, then speaks back numbered first-aid steps. Nothing is sent anywhere. There is no network to send it through.

Where the project stands

  • Working

    The software chain runs end to end, offline, on one laptop. Sensor readings arrive, the model reads them, and its answer changes because of them.

    Verified 22 July 2026 against a local model. The receipts are on the Proof page.

  • Working

    The firmware compiles for the real chip and its sensor maths passes tests against the manufacturer's own reference numbers.

    6 checks on the pressure maths, 3 on fall detection, 39 on the software that talks to the model.

  • Stands in

    A laptop does the job of the pack computer. A cable does the job of the wireless link. Both are deliberate substitutions, and both are labelled everywhere they appear.

  • Simulated

    Every sensor reading on this site is generated rather than measured, and says so wherever it appears. Speech-to-text and wound-photo analysis are placeholders that do nothing yet.

Every claim here points at a file in the public repository. If you find one that does not, that is a bug worth telling me about.

The whole system is held together by one line of text

Ten times a second, the wrist unit prints a single line describing everything it can currently sense. That line is the only agreement between the parts, which means any one of them can be swapped out without touching the others, and you can read it with your own eyes while debugging. Here is a real one, from the moment of a fall:

{"t_ms":300,
 "max30102":{"ok":true,"red":10249,"ir":10398},
 "bmp280":{"ok":true,"temp_c":21.0,"press_pa":101300.2,"alt_m":2.1},
 "lis3dh":{"ok":true,"x_g":1.80,"y_g":1.50,"z_g":1.20,"mag_g":2.62},
 "fall_detected":true}

In plain English

Three tenths of a second after switching on: the optical sensor is working and returning these two light readings. The pressure sensor is working, it is 21 °C, and you are 2.1 metres above where you started. The accelerometer is working and just felt 2.62 g, which is more than two and a half times normal gravity. Putting those last numbers together: that looked like a fall.

Two details in there carry most of the design. Each sensor reports its own "ok" first, so a sensor that has failed says so instead of quietly reporting zero. A fabricated zero is more dangerous than a blank, because someone might act on it.

And the two light readings stay exactly what they are: raw light readings. Turning them into a heart rate takes signal processing this project does not do, so this project does not show a heart rate anywhere.

Live in this page

Drive the wrist unit yourself

This is the real thing running in your browser, not a video. Move the sliders and watch the readings change. Press Simulate fall and watch the last value flip. Switch a sensor off and watch the line report the failure instead of inventing a number.

It runs the same altitude and fall-detection formulas as the firmware, with the same constants, and prints the identical line format the board would print. That is why the rest of the system cannot tell the difference.

Label

This is a simulator. No sensor is attached to your computer, and the panel says so itself. What it demonstrates is the data format and the maths, not hardware.

Ghost Medic is an engineering prototype built by one self-taught person. It is not a medical device, it has not been clinically evaluated, and nothing it says should be treated as medical advice. Simulated data is labelled simulated. Placeholders are labelled placeholders. There is no heart rate or blood-oxygen reading anywhere in this project, by design.
