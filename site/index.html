Prototype

A first-aid assistant that works with no signal.

Ghost Medic is a prototype for people who are hours from help with no connection. A wrist unit tracks movement and the air around you. A language model on hardware in your own pack reads those numbers together with what you tell it, then speaks numbered first-aid steps. Nothing is sent anywhere because there is no network.

Where the project stands

  • Working

    The software chain runs end to end, offline, on one laptop: sensor readings arrive and the model's answer changes because of them.

    Verified 22 July 2026 against a local model. The receipts are on the Proof page.

  • Working

    The firmware compiles for the real chip and its maths passes tests against the manufacturer's own reference numbers: 6 + 3 checks, plus 39 on the software that talks to the model.

  • Stands in

    A laptop does the job of the pack computer. A cable does the job of the wireless link. Both are deliberate substitutions, and both are labelled everywhere they appear.

  • Simulated

    Every sensor reading on this site is generated rather than measured, and says so wherever it appears. Speech-to-text and wound-photo analysis are placeholders that do nothing yet.

Every claim here points at a file in the public repository. If you find one that does not, that is a bug worth telling me about.

The whole system is held together by one line of text

Ten times a second the wrist unit prints one line describing everything it can sense. That line is the only agreement between the parts, so any part can be swapped without touching the others. Here is a real one, from the moment of a fall:

{"t_ms":300,
 "max30102":{"ok":true,"red":10249,"ir":10398},
 "bmp280":{"ok":true,"temp_c":21.0,"press_pa":101300.2,"alt_m":2.1},
 "lis3dh":{"ok":true,"x_g":1.80,"y_g":1.50,"z_g":1.20,"mag_g":2.62},
 "fall_detected":true}

In plain English

Three tenths of a second after switching on: the optical sensor is working and returning these two light readings. The pressure sensor is working, it is 21 °C, and you are 2.1 metres above where you started. The accelerometer is working and just felt 2.62 g, which is more than two and a half times normal gravity. Putting those last numbers together: that looked like a fall.

Two details carry most of the design. Each sensor reports its own "ok" first, so a failed sensor says so instead of quietly reporting zero. And the light readings stay raw light readings: turning them into a heart rate takes signal processing this project does not do, so no heart rate appears anywhere.

Ghost Medic is an engineering prototype built by one self-taught person. It is not a medical device, it has not been clinically evaluated, and nothing it says should be treated as medical advice.
