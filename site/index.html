Prototype

A first-aid assistant that works with no signal.

A wrist unit reads your motion and the air around you. A language model on hardware you carry turns that, and what you say, into spoken first-aid steps. No network.

Where the project stands

  • Working

    The software chain runs end to end, offline, on one laptop: sensor readings change the model's answer. Verified 22 Jul 2026 against a local model, receipts on Proof.

  • Working

    Firmware compiles for the real chip; its maths passes the manufacturer's own reference numbers (6 + 3 checks), plus 39 on the model-facing software.

  • Stands in

    A laptop stands in for the pack computer, a cable for the wireless link. Labelled wherever they appear.

  • Simulated

    Every sensor reading here is generated, not measured. Speech-to-text and wound-photo analysis are placeholders that do nothing yet.

Every claim points at a file in the repository.

The product, as a concept

What it would look like on you

  • 1
    Wrist case. Holds the sensor board and firmware, both real: the board hand-routed in KiCad, the firmware compiled with its maths tested.
  • 2
    Display. Concept only: the built board has no screen. Today the app shows the readings and speaks the steps.
  • 3
    The cable. Real bridge software, carrying every reading unchanged: a wired stand-in for the eventual wrist-to-pack BLE link.
  • 4
    Belt pack. Compute and battery in one curved case, sized around a Jetson Orin Nano class module (7 to 15 W on paper). No module has been selected and nothing has been measured. The offline LLM layer is real; a laptop stands in today.

A concept, built in code rather than CAD. The only hardware designed is the circuit board, hand-routed in KiCad, on the Hardware page. Drag to rotate, scroll to zoom.

The whole system is held together by one line of text

Ten times a second the wrist unit prints one line describing everything it senses. It is the only contract between the parts. Here is one, from a fall:

{"t_ms":300,
 "max30102":{"ok":true,"red":10249,"ir":10398},
 "bmp280":{"ok":true,"temp_c":21.0,"press_pa":101300.2,"alt_m":2.1},
 "lis3dh":{"ok":true,"x_g":1.80,"y_g":1.50,"z_g":1.20,"mag_g":2.62},
 "fall_detected":true}

In plain English

Optical sensor working, two light readings. Pressure working: 21 °C, 2.1 m above the start. Accelerometer just felt 2.62 g, over 2.5x gravity. That looked like a fall.

Each sensor reports its own "ok" first, so a failure says so instead of quietly reading zero. The light stays raw: no heart rate is derived anywhere, by design.
